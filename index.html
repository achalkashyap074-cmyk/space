ABOUT SPACE

Space — the final frontier — is an almost incomprehensibly vast expanse that stretches far beyond anything the human mind can truly grasp. It begins roughly 100 kilometres above Earth's surface, where the atmosphere fades into the silent vacuum of the cosmos, and extends outward for at least 93 billion light-years in every direction across the observable universe. Within this staggering volume exist more stars than grains of sand on all of Earth's beaches combined — estimated at over a septillion (10²⁴). Planets, moons, asteroids, comets, nebulae, black holes, neutron stars, and entire galaxies spiral through the dark in a grand cosmic dance governed by gravity, electromagnetism, and the fundamental forces of physics. Our own Solar System — a tiny speck in the Milky Way galaxy — contains eight planets, hundreds of moons, and a dazzling asteroid belt, yet it represents an almost negligible fraction of the known universe. Beyond our galaxy lie over two trillion more galaxies, each home to billions of stars and planetary systems, many of which may harbour conditions suitable for life. Space is not merely empty darkness; it is threaded with cosmic microwave background radiation, dark matter, and dark energy — the invisible scaffolding that shapes the large-scale structure of everything that exists. To study space is to study the origin, nature, and destiny of the universe itself — and perhaps, one day, to discover that we are not alone within it.

Beyond Our Solar System

Stars & Constellations

The closest star system to Earth is Alpha Centauri, 4.37 light-years away. Our night sky contains 88 officially recognised constellations with billions of stars.

The Milky Way

Our home galaxy is a barred spiral spanning 100,000 light-years across, containing an estimated 100–400 billion stars and at least as many planets.

The Multiverse

Theoretical physics suggests our universe may be one of infinite universes comprising a multiverse, each governed by different physical laws and constants.
